Thermodynamics

← Kinetics
Thermodynamics →

Thermodynamics (or chemical thermodynamics) is the study of the interrelation of heat and work with chemical reactions or with physical changes of state within the confines of the laws of thermodynamics.

Some changes seem to happen in one direction but not the other. For example, a fuel burns in air and forms CO2 and H2O, but those products will never react to re-form the fuel and oxygen. This is an example of spontaneous change.

The Second Law of Thermodynamics

The disorder or entropy (S) of the universe is spontaneous. For any real spontaneous process,

$$S_\text{universe} = S_\text{system} + S_\text{surroundings} > 0 \tag{1}$$
Determine which has higher entropy.
  1. 1 mol of SO2 (g) or 1 mol of SO3 (g)
  2. seawater at 2°C or seawater at 23°C
  3. 1 mol of CO2 (s) or 1 mol of CO2 (g)
  4. 1 mol CF4 or 1 mol of CCl4
Show solution
  1. 1 mol of SO3 (g). For equal numbers of moles of substances with the same types of atoms in the same physical state, the more atoms in the molecule, the more types of motion available, and thus the higher the entropy.
  2. seawater at 23°C. Entropy increases with rising temperature.
  3. 1 mol of CO2 (g). For a given substance, entropy increases in the sequence s < l < g.
  4. 1 mol of CCl4. For similar compounds, entropy increases with molar mass.

Calculating Change in Entropy

The standard entropy of reaction, \(\pu{\Delta S^o_{rxn}}\), is the entropy change that occurs when all reactants and products are in their standard states.

In predicting the sign of \(\pu{\Delta S^o_{rxn}}\), the change in the amount (mol) of gases are important. If the number of moles of gas increases, \(\pu{\Delta S^o_{rxn}}\) is positive; if the number decreases, \(\pu{\Delta S^o_{rxn}}\) is negative.

$$\pu{\Delta S^o_{rxn} = \sum mS^o_{products} - \sum nS^o_{reactants}} \tag{2}$$
Predict the sign of \(\pu{\Delta S^o_{rxn}}\) and calculate its value. $$\ce{N2H4 (l) + O2 (g) -> N2 (g) + 2H2O (g)}$$
Show solution
Since the amount of gas increases (1 mol to 3 mol), entropy should increase (\(\pu{\Delta S^o_{rxn}} > 0\)). Using Equation 2, $$\begin{align*} \pu{\Delta S^o_{rxn}} &= \pu{\sum mS^o_{products} - \sum nS^o_{reactants}} \\ &= [\ce{N2}+\ce{H2O}] - [\ce{N2H4} + \ce{O2}] \\ &= [(\pu{1 mol})(\pu{191.5J/mol.K})+(\pu{2 mol})(\pu{188.72 J/mol.K})]\\ &\quad\ - [(\pu{1 mol})(\ce{121.2 J/mol.K}) + (\pu{1 mol})(\ce{205 J/mol.K})] \\ &= [\pu{191.5 J/K} + \pu{377.44 J/K}] -[\pu{121.2 J/K} + \pu{205 J/K}] \\ \pu{\Delta S^o_{rxn}} &= \pu{242.74 J/K} \end{align*}$$ Thus, the standard entropy change is 242.74 J/K.

Entropy Changes in Surroundings

$$\Delta S_{\text{universe}} = \Delta S_{\text{system}} + \Delta S_{\text{surroundings}} \tag{3}$$

In an exothermic change, heat released by the system is absorbed by the surroundings. More heat increases the freedom of motion of the particles and makes the energy more dispersed, so the entropy of the surroundings increases.

$$q_{\text{sys}} < 0,\quad q_{\text{surr}} > 0,\ \text{and}\ \Delta S_{\text{surr}} > 0$$

In an endothermic change, heat absorbed by the system is released by the surroundings. Less heat reduces the freedom of motion of the particles and makes the energy less dispersed, so the entropy of the surroundings decreases.

$$q_{\text{sys}} > 0,\quad q_{\text{surr}} < 0,\ \text{and}\ \Delta S_{\text{surr}} < 0$$

ΔSsurr is directly related to an opposite change in the heat of the system.

$$\Delta S_{\text{surr}} = -\frac{\Delta H_{\text{sys}}}{T} \tag{4}$$
At 298K, the formation of ammonia has a negative \(\Delta S_{\text{sys}}\): $$\ce{N2 (g) + 3H2 (g) -> 2NH3 (g) \qquad \Delta S^{\text{o}}_{\text{sys}} = -197 J/K}$$ Calculate \(\Delta S_{\text{univ}}\) and state whether the reaction occurs spontaneously at this temperature.
Show solution
Referring to equation 3 and 4, $$\Delta S_{\text{univ}} &= \Delta S_{\text{sys}} + \Delta S_{\text{surr}} = \Delta S_{\text{sys}} - \frac{\Delta H_{\text{sys}}}{T}$$ For enthalpy (ΔHsys): $$\begin{align*} \Delta H^{\text{o}}_{\text{sys}} &= \Delta H^{\text{o}}_{\text{rxn}} \\ &= \ce{[(2 mol NH3)(-45.9 kJ/mol)] - [(3 mol H2)(0 kJ/mol) + (1 mol N2)(0 kJ/mol)]} \\ \Delta H^{\text{o}}_{\text{sys}} &= \pu{-91.8 kJ} \end{align*}$$ -91.8 kJ can then be substituted in the equation. $$\begin{align*} \Delta S_{\text{univ}} &= \Delta S_{\text{sys}} - \frac{\Delta H_{\text{sys}}}{T} \\ &= \ce{-197 J/K - \frac{-91.8 kJ(\frac{1000 J}{1 kJ})}{298K}} \\ &= \ce{111 J/K} \end{align*}$$ Therefore, ΔSuniv is 111 J/K. Since ΔSuniv > 0, the reaction occurs spontaneously.

Entropy Change at Equilibrium State

When the process reaches equilibrium, there is no further net change, ΔSuniv = 0, because any entropy change in the system is balanced by an opposite entropy change in the surroundings.

$$\Delta S_{\text{univ}} = \Delta S_{\text{sys}} + \Delta S_{\text{surr}} = 0 \\ \Delta S_{\text{sys}} = -\Delta S_{\text{surr}}$$

Entropy, Free Energy, and Work

The Gibbs free energy, or simply free energy (G), combines the system’s enthalpy and entropy:

$$G = H - TS \tag{5}$$

Deriving the Gibbs Equation

From the second law,

$$\Delta S_{\text{univ}} = \Delta S_{\text{sys}} + \Delta S_{\text{surr}}$$

Substituting ΔSsurr, gives a relationship that solely relies on the system.

$$\Delta S_{\text{univ}} = \Delta S_{\text{sys}} - \frac{\Delta H_{\text{sys}}}{T}$$

Multiplying both sides by -T gives:

$$-T\Delta S_{\text{univ}} = \Delta H_{\text{sys}} - T\Delta S_{\text{sys}}$$

We have now derived Gibbs equation.

$$\Delta G_{\text{sys}} = \Delta H_{\text{sys}} - T\Delta S_{\text{sys}} \tag{6}$$
