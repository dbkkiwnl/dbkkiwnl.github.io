Chemical kinetics studies the rate at which a chemical process occurs.

Factors Affecting Rate of Reaction

Concentration. A reaction can occur only if the reactant molecules collide. The more molecules present in the container, the more frequently they collide, and so the more often they react.

Rate ∝ collision energy ∝ concentration

Surface area. The greater its surface area, the more contact it makes with the other reactant, and the faster the reaction occurs. For example, in burning of a solid fuel like wood, increasing the surface area by chopping wood into smaller pieces makes it combust faster.

Temperature.

Energy of collisions.

Reaction Rate

The speed of a chemical reaction, or its rate, is defined as:

$$\text{rate} = \frac{\Delta \text{concentration}}{\Delta \text{time}} \tag{1}$$

In general, a reaction in the form \(\ce{aA + bB -> cC + dD}\), the rate can be expressed as:

$$\text{rate} = -\frac{1}{a}\frac{\Delta [A]}{\Delta t} = -\frac{1}{b}\frac{\Delta [B]}{\Delta t} = \frac{1}{c}\frac{\Delta [C]}{\Delta t} = \frac{1}{d}\frac{\Delta [D]}{\Delta t} \tag{2}$$

As the reactants decrease, the products increase. Observe that the signs for the reactants A and B are negative, while the products C and D are positive.

Express the rate in terms of changes for the entire chemical equation. $$\ce{H2 (g) + I2 (g) -> 2HI (g)}$$
Show solution
The rate of the equation above is: $$\text{rate} = -\frac{\Delta \ce{[H2]}}{\Delta t} = -\frac{\Delta \ce{[I2]}}{\Delta t} = \frac{\Delta \ce{[HI]}}{2 \Delta t}$$
Hydrogen gas has a nonpolluting combustion product (water vapor). It is used as a fuel aboard the space shuttle and in earthbound cars with prototype engines. $$\ce{2H2 (g) + O2 (g) -> 2H2O (g)}$$ (a) Express the rate in terms of changes in [H2], [O2], [H2O] with time.
(b) When [O2] is decreasing at 0.23 mol/L⋅s, at what rate is [H2O] increasing?
Show solution
(a) Referring to Equation 2, $$\text{rate} = -\frac{\Delta \ce{[H2]}}{2 \Delta t} = -\frac{\Delta \ce{[O2]}}{\Delta t} = \frac{1}{2}\frac{\Delta \ce{[H2O]}}{\Delta t}$$ (b) Since the rates of the chemical equation are all equal to each other, the rate of [O2] is half of the rate of [H2O]. Since [O2] is decreasing, it is negative: -0.23 mol/L⋅s. $$\begin{align*} \frac{1}{2} \frac{\Delta \ce{[H2O]}}{\Delta t} &= -\frac{\Delta \ce{[O2]}}{\Delta t} \\ \frac{1}{2} \frac{\Delta \ce{[H2O]}}{\Delta t} &= -(\pu{-0.23 mol/L*s}) \\ \frac{\Delta \ce{[H2O]}}{\Delta t} &= 2(\pu{0.23 mol/L*s}) \\ \frac{\Delta \ce{[H2O]}}{\Delta t} &= \pu{0.46 mol/L*s} \\ \end{align*}$$ Therefore, [H2O] is increasing at a rate of 0.46 mol/L⋅s.
