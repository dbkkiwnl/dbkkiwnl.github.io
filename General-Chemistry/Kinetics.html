Chemical kinetics studies the rate at which a chemical process occurs.

Factors Affecting Rate of Reaction

Concentration. A reaction can occur only if the reactant molecules collide. The more molecules present in the container, the more frequently they collide, and so the more often they react.

Rate ∝ collision energy ∝ concentration

Surface area. The greater its surface area, the more contact it makes with the other reactant, and the faster the reaction occurs. For example, in burning of a solid fuel like wood, increasing the surface area by chopping wood into smaller pieces makes it combust faster.

Temperature.

Energy of collisions.

Reaction Rate

The speed of a chemical reaction, or its rate, is defined as:

$$\text{rate} = \frac{\Delta \text{concentration}}{\Delta \text{time}} \tag{1}$$

In general, a reaction in the form \(\ce{aA + bB -> cC + dD}\), the rate can be expressed as:

$$\text{rate} = -\frac{1}{a}\frac{\Delta [A]}{\Delta t} = -\frac{1}{b}\frac{\Delta [B]}{\Delta t} = \frac{1}{c}\frac{\Delta [C]}{\Delta t} = \frac{1}{d}\frac{\Delta [D]}{\Delta t} \tag{2}$$

As the reactants decrease, the products increase. Observe that the signs for the reactants A and B are negative, while the products C and D are positive.

Express the rate in terms of changes for the entire chemical equation. $$\ce{H2 (g) + I2 (g) -> 2HI (g)}$$
Show solution
The rate of the equation above is: $$\text{rate} = -\frac{\Delta \ce{[H2]}}{\Delta t} = -\frac{\Delta \ce{[I2]}}{\Delta t} = \frac{\Delta \ce{[HI]}}{2 \Delta t}$$
Hydrogen gas has a nonpolluting combustion product (water vapor). It is used as a fuel aboard the space shuttle and in earthbound cars with prototype engines. $$\ce{2H2 (g) + O2 (g) -> 2H2O (g)}$$ (a) Express the rate in terms of changes in [H2], [O2], [H2O] with time.
(b) When [O2] is decreasing at 0.23 mol/L⋅s, at what rate is [H2O] increasing?
Show solution
(a) Referring to Equation 2, $$\text{rate} = -\frac{\Delta \ce{[H2]}}{2 \Delta t} = -\frac{\Delta \ce{[O2]}}{\Delta t} = \frac{1}{2}\frac{\Delta \ce{[H2O]}}{\Delta t}$$ (b) Since the rates of the chemical equation are all equal to each other, the rate of [O2] is half of the rate of [H2O]. Since [O2] is decreasing, it is negative: -0.23 mol/L⋅s. $$\begin{align*} \frac{1}{2} \frac{\Delta \ce{[H2O]}}{\Delta t} &= -\frac{\Delta \ce{[O2]}}{\Delta t} \\ \frac{1}{2} \frac{\Delta \ce{[H2O]}}{\Delta t} &= -(\pu{-0.23 mol/L*s}) \\ \frac{\Delta \ce{[H2O]}}{\Delta t} &= 2(\pu{0.23 mol/L*s}) \\ \frac{\Delta \ce{[H2O]}}{\Delta t} &= \pu{0.46 mol/L*s} \\ \end{align*}$$ Therefore, [H2O] is increasing at a rate of 0.46 mol/L⋅s.

The Rate Law and Its Components

For a reaction occuring at a fixed temperature, \(\ce{aA + bB + \cdots -> cC + dD \cdots}\), its rate law is:

$$\text{rate} = k\ce{[A]}^m\ce{[B]}^n \cdots$$

wherein k is called the rate constant, and m and n are the reaction orders. A reaction has an individual order with respect to each reactant, and and overall reaction order.

Note that the balancing coefficients in the equation (a and b) are not necessarily related to m and n. The components of the rate law are only found by experiment.

For example, in a reaction with a single reactant \(\ce{A -> products}\), the following are the possible reaction orders:

Table 1. Rate laws for a single reactant.
Reaction order Rate law
zero order \(\text{rate} = k\ce{[A]}^0 = k\)
first order \(\text{rate} = k\ce{[A]}^1 = k\ce{[A]}\)
second order \(\text{rate} = k\ce{[A]}^2\)

In summary, the reaction orders are the exponents, and the overall reaction order is determined by adding each individual reaction order.

For the following reaction, use the given rate law to determine the reaction order with respect to each reactant and the overall order: $$\ce{2NO (g) + O2 (g) -> 2NO2 (g)};\ \text{rate} = k\ce{[NO]^2[O2]}$$
Show solution
The reaction order is obtained from the exponents of the rate, not from the coefficients of the chemical equation. Since [NO]'s exponent is 2 and [O2]'s is 1, the reaction orders are thus second order with respect to NO, first order with respect to O2, and the overall reaction order is third order.

Reaction orders can also be determined from experiments.

For the reaction \(\ce{A + 2B -> C + D}\), its rate law can be generally expressed as \(\text{rate} = k\ce{[A]}^m\ce{[B]}^n\). Given the following table of experiments, find the individual reaction orders with respect to A and B.
Experiment Initial rate (mol/L⋅s) Initial [A] (mol/L) Initial [B] (mol/L)
1 1.75 × 10-3 2.50 × 10-2 3.00 × 10-2
2 3.50 × 10-3 5.00 × 10-2 3.00 × 10-2
3 3.50 × 10-3 2.50 × 10-2 6.00 × 10-2
4 7.00 × 10-3 5.00 × 10-2 6.00 × 10-2
Show solution
Finding order m with respect to A. To find m, two experiments need to be compared. In this case, experiment 1 and 2 can be compared. $$\frac{\text{rate 1}}{\text{rate 2}} = \frac{k\ce{[A]^m_1[B]^n_1}}{k\ce{[A]^m_2[B]^n_2}} = \frac{\ce{[A]^m_1}}{\ce{[A]^m_2}} = \left(\frac{\ce{[A]_1}}{\ce{[A]_2}}\right)^m$$ Substituting the actual values, $$\begin{align*} \frac{\pu{1.75 \times 10^{-3} mol/L*s}}{\pu{3.50 \times 10^{-3} mol/L*s}} &= \left(\frac{\pu{2.50 \times 10^{-2} mol/L}}{\pu{5.00 \times 10^{-2} mol/L}}\right)^m \\ 0.5 &= 0.5^m \\ m &= 1 \end{align*}$$ Finding order n with respect to B. Similarly, experiments 1 and 3 can be used. $$\frac{\text{rate 1}}{\text{rate 3}} = \frac{k\ce{[A]^m_1[B]^n_1}}{k\ce{[A]^m_3[B]^n_3}} = \frac{\ce{[A]^n_1}}{\ce{[A]^n_3}} = \left(\frac{\ce{[A]_1}}{\ce{[A]_3}}\right)^n$$ Substitute. $$\begin{align*} \frac{\pu{1.75 \times 10^{-3} mol/L*s}}{\pu{3.50 \times 10^{-3} mol/L*s}} &= \left(\frac{\pu{3.00 \times 10^{-2} mol/L}}{\pu{6.00 \times 10^{-2} mol/L}}\right)^n \\ 0.5 &= 0.5^n \\ n &= 1 \end{align*}$$ Thus, the reaction orders are first order with respect to A and first order with respect to B.

Integrated Rate Laws

Table 2. Integrated rate laws.
Reaction order Rate law
zero order \(\ce{[A]_t - [A]_0}=-kt\)
first order \(\ce{ln\frac{[A]_0}{[A]_t}}=kt\)
second order \(\ce{\frac{1}{[A]_t} - \frac{1}{[A]_0}}=kt\)
At 1000°C, cyclobutane (C4H8)decomposes in a first-order reaction, with the very high rate constant of 87 s-1, to two molecules of ethylene. The initial cyclobutane concentration is 2.00 M. What is the concentration after 0.010 seconds?
Show solution
Since it is a first order reaction, the following formula is used. $$\ce{ln\frac{[C_4H_8]_0}{[C_4H_8]_t}}=kt$$ Knowing that \(\pu{k = 87 s-1}\), \(\pu{t = 0.010 s}\), \(\ce{[C_4H_8]_0 = 2.00M}\), substitute. $$\begin{align*} \ln \frac{\pu{2.00mol/L}}{\ce{[C_4H_8]_t}} &= (\pu{87s-1})(\pu{0.0010s}) \\ \ln \frac{\pu{2.00mol/L}}{\ce{[C_4H_8]_t}} &= (0.87) \\ \cancel{e}^{\cancel{\ln} \frac{\pu{2.00mol/L}}{\ce{[C_4H_8]_t}}} &= e^{0.87} \\ \frac{\pu{2.00mol/L}}{\ce{[C_4H_8]_t}} &= e^{0.87} \\ \ce{[C_4H_8]_t} &= \frac{\pu{2.00mol/L}}{e^{0.87}} \\ \ce{[C_4H_8]_t}&= \pu{0.84 mol/L} \end{align*}$$ Thus, the concentration of the cyclobutane after 0.010 seconds is 0.84 mol/L.

Reaction Half-Life

Table 3. Reaction half-life laws.
Reaction order Rate law
zero order \(t_{1/2} = \frac{\ce{[A]_0}}{2k}\)
first order \(t_{1/2} = \frac{\ln 2}{k}\)
second order \(t_{1/2} = \frac{1}{k\ce{[A]_0}}\)
Cyclopropane is the smallest cyclic hydrocarbon. Because its 60° bond angles reduce orbital overlap, its bonds are weak. As a resut, it is thermally unstable and rearranges to propene at 1000°C by a first-order reaction. The rate constant is 9.2 s-1. What is the half-life of the reaction?
Show solution
Since it is a first order reaction, $$\begin{align*} t_{1/2} &= \frac{\ln 2}{k} \\ t_{1/2}&= \frac{\ln 2}{\pu{9.2s-1}} \\ t_{1/2}&= \pu{0.075s} \end{align*}$$ Thus, the half-life of the reaction is 0.075 seconds. It takes 0.075 seconds for half the cyclopropane to form propene.
