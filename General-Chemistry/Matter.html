Matter and Its Properties

Beginning
Measurements →

As a general starting point, chemistry is the study of matter and its properties, the changes that matter undergoes, and the energy associated with those changes.

Matter is anything that occupies space and has mass. It is composed of tiny particles called atoms.

States of Matter

Each state of matter is defined by how the sample fills a container.

  • A solid has a fixed shape that does not conform to its container. It is not defined by rigidity and hardness.
  • A liquid has a varying shape that does conform to its container, only to the extent of its volume; liquids have an upper surface.
  • A gas also has a varying shapethat conforms to its container, filling the entire container; having no surface.

Properties of Matter

Physical properties are characteristics a substance shows by itself, without changing into or interacting with another substance. It can be either intensive or extensive.

  • Intensive properties are independent of the amount of matter present (pressure, temperature).
  • Extensive properties are dependent on the amount of matter present (mass, volume).

Chemical properties are characteristics a substance shows as it changes into or interacts with another substance. As an example, observe Table 1.

Table 1. Characteristic properties of copper.
Physical properties Chemical properties
Easily shaped into sheets and wires Slowly forms a blue-green carbonate in moist air
Has a density of 8.95 g/cm3 Reacts with nitric or sulfuric acid
Has a melting point of 1083 degrees Celsius Slowly forms deep-blue solution in aqueous ammonia

Classification of Matter

Matter is classified into either a pure substance or a mixture.

A pure substance can be an element or compound. While an element cannot be separated into simpler substances by chemical methods (eg. \(\ce{O}\)), a compound is composed of two or more elements in fixed proportions and can be separated by chemical methods (eg. \(\ce{CO2}\)).

Mixtures consist of two or more pure substances that retain their individual identities and can be separated by physical methods. Homogeneous mixtures have a uniform composition and properties, while heteregeneous mixtures are not uniform.

Table 2. Examples of pure substances and mixtures.
Pure substances Mixtures
Table salt Iodized salt
Table sugar Distilled water

Methods of Separating Mixtures

  • Evaporation is a method used to separate a mixture of a liquid with a dissolved solid, evaporating a liquid and leaving behind a solid.
  • Distillation is a separation technique used to separate components of a liquid mixture by a process of heating and cooling.
  • Filtration is a technique used to separate the components of a mixture containing an undissolved solid in a liquid by using a funnel with filter paper to retain the solids while letting the liquid through.
  • Chromatography is a separation method based on how different components in a mixture have different affinities for stationary and mobile phases.
