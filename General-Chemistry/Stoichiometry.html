Stoichiometry comes from two Greek words: stoicheion (element) and metron (measure). As Jeremias Richter wrote in 1792, "Stoichiometry is the science of measuring the quantitative proportions or mass ratios in which chemical elements stand to one another."

The Mole and Molar Mass

The mole (symbol mol) is one of the most important units in chemistry. It is a unit of measurement for an amount of substance.

$$1\ \text{mol} = 6.022 \times 10^{23}\ \text{entities} \tag{1}$$

Note that the term entities can be atoms, molecules, ions, formula units, etc.

The number 6.022 × 1023 mol-1 is called Avogadro's number. It is often denoted as \(N_A\).

How many atoms are present in 2.14 moles of Vanadium?
We can use \(N_A\) to convert moles into atoms. $$2.14\ \text{mol V} \left( \frac{6.022 \times 10^{23}\ \text{atoms V}}{1\ \text{mol V}} \right) = 1.29 \times 10^{24}\ \text{atoms V}$$ Thus, there are 1.29 × 1024 atoms of Vanadium in 2.14 moles of it.

The molar mass of any given substance is the mass of one mole of that substance. It is numerically equal to the atomic mass or atomic weight. For example, hydrogen's atomic weight, and thus its molar mass is 1.01 μ or 1.01 g/mol.

For future usage, all atomic weights of elements are to be rounded to two decimal places.

Calculate the molar mass of CH4.
We can solve for the molar mass by adding up each individual molar mass of the elements, in this case, carbon and hydrogen. The subscript 4 indicates that there are 4 hydrogen atoms. This becomes the multiple of its molar mass. $$\begin{align} &\text{C:}\ 1 \times \pu{12.01 g/mol} = \pu{12.01 g/mol}\\ +\quad &\text{H:}\ 4 \times \pu{1.01 g/mol} = \pu{4.04 g/mol}\\ \hline &\text{Molar mass} = \pu{16.05 g/mol} \end{align}$$ Thus the molar mass of CH4 is 16.05 g/mol.

Building on the previous two concepts learned, we can now solve for problems asking for grams from moles.

What is the mass of 0.25 mol of CH4?
In stoichiometry, dimensional analysis is commonly performed. As such, let us analyze the problem. Given the mole value, the mass (presumably in grams) is asked. Thus, the molar mass can be used. $$\pu{0.25 mol \ce{CH4}} \left( \frac{\pu{16.05 g}}{\pu{1 mol}}\ \ce{CH4} \right) = \pu{4.0 g \ce{CH4}}$$ Recall significant figures. Thus, the mass of 0.25 of CH4 is 4.0 grams.
